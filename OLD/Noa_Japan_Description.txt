## **Japan Travel Guide: Insights from a 5-Month Journey**

This guide is based on my (Adi's) experiences and advice shared with a friend planning her trip to Japan. I spent five months exploring the country, including two months living in Tokyo.

---

### **1. General Tips for Solo Travel (טיול לבד בכללי)**
*Tags: #solotravel #alone #friends #mindset*

*   **Mindset is Key:** It's great to go into solo travel with a good mindset, being aware of what you expect from the experience.
*   **Getting Used to Solitude:** You can get very accustomed to traveling alone. After two months solo, I found it a bit overwhelming to travel with a friend, even one I love.
*   **Reuniting with Others:** Be prepared for an adjustment period if you meet up with friends or family after a significant time traveling solo. There can be common "symptoms" or readjustments in the dynamic.

---

### **2. Tips for Solo Travel in Japan (טיפים לטיול לבד ביפן)**
*Tags: #solojapan #meetingpeople #hostels #BeitHaSamurai #israeli #social*

*   **Tourist Demographics:** Most tourists in Japan are there for 3 weeks to a month, often sticking to the "Golden Triangle" (Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka).
*   **Beyond the Golden Triangle:** Outside this main route, you'll still meet tourists, but they are more likely to be Asian, particularly Chinese (often due to budget).
*   **Meeting People - Accommodation Choices:**
    *   **Beit HaSamurai (The Samurai House) in Tokyo:** While a great, affordable option with a lovely host family (more details under "Tokyo"), it primarily houses Israelis, often post-army. If your goal is to meet a diverse group of travelers or locals, this might not be the best primary base.
    *   **Hostels in Central Areas:** To meet other international travelers (e.g., Europeans) and have spontaneous experiences like going to karaoke, consider staying in hostels in central areas like Shinjuku in Tokyo.
*   **Context for Accommodation:** My extended stay at Beit HaSamurai was after I had already traveled solo for two months and my primary goal was to spend time with the host family, not necessarily to meet new travelers.
*   **Being Israeli:**
    *   My trip was after October 7th (following five months of reserve duty).
    *   When people asked where I was from, I told them I was Israeli.
    *   **Frankly, nobody cares.** It was not an issue.

### **3. Japan: Places and Weather (יפן מקומות במזג אוויר)**
*Tags: #weather #hokkaido #alps #yakushima #kyoto #osaka #ishigaki #okinawa #sakura #trekking #driving #carrental #transportation #bullettrain #flights #itinerary*

*   **General Weather (July/August):**
    *   July/August is a **very tough** time to travel in most of Japan due to extreme heat and humidity.
    *   The humidity is often more challenging than the temperature itself.
    *   It can rain in any season in Japan.
*   **My Travel Route (for context):** I started in southern Japan in late March and moved north with the Sakura (cherry blossoms) to stay in pleasant weather, reaching Tokyo. This was based on a friend's itinerary.
*   **Okinawa & Ishigaki (Southern Islands):**
    *   The island of **Ishigaki** (near Okinawa) could be a relevant option.
    *   The rainy season there tends to end earlier than on the mainland.
    *   Being coastal, the heat might be more manageable.
    *   It has a completely different vibe and even different food from mainland Japan.
*   **Southern Japan (Kyushu, Yakushima):**
    *   **Yakushima Island:**
        *   My absolute favorite place in Japan.
        *   Incredible, unique nature (inspiration for Princess Mononoke, though I haven't seen the film). It feels like nature has taken over.
        *   It rains on average 2-3 days a week year-round, so everything is covered in lush moss. Giant trees with entirely green trunks.
        *   Checked July weather: seemed "not terrible" (around 21°C), but expect high humidity. Being amongst trees might offer some coolness.
    *   The southern region (and Hokkaido) is amazing for **treks** and significantly less touristy.
*   **Kyoto & Osaka (Golden Triangle):**
    *   I found Kyoto during peak Sakura season to be unbearably crowded; you couldn't even see the ground.
    *   **Personally, I feel skipping Kyoto and Osaka is not a big loss.** They were among my least favorite places in Japan.
*   **Japanese Alps (Central Honshu):**
    *   Very relevant for trekking, especially in summer when it's cooler in the mountains than in the cities.
    *   I aimed to trek there but arrived in the off-season (early spring), so only one path was open due to snow higher up.
    *   **Recommendation for July/August:** Consider starting in Hokkaido, and if/when Tokyo becomes unbearable, escape to the Alps for about a week of trekking.
*   **Hokkaido (Northern Island):**
    *   Ideal seasons are **summer** (for blooming flowers and pleasant trekking weather) or **winter** (for snow). July/August is a good time.
    *   **Sapporo:** A great city to experience a different side of Japan (non-Tokyo) in potentially more comfortable weather. I really liked it.
    *   I was there in early May (off-season for summer activities), it was cold, we wore thermals and 3 layers daily, and it even snowed in the city.
    *   **Transportation:** Public transport for touring Hokkaido is not as convenient as in the rest of Japan.
        *   **Car Rental:** Highly recommended. I was prepared to rent a car even if I were solo.
            *   For two people, renting a car can be almost the same cost as relying on public transport. Fuel is not overly expensive.
            *   Look into **local rental companies**; they can be cheaper than major international ones found on sites like Rentalcars.com. I found a car in southern Japan for 100 NIS/day through a local company when everything else was booked.
            *   **Driving License:** Get an International Driving Permit (IDP). It's easy and cheap (e.g., 18 NIS at Optica Halperin in Israel, takes minutes). Check age restrictions for renting (23 might be okay, but insurance could be slightly higher than for 24+).
            *   **Driving Experience:** Driving in Hokkaido is amazing. Japanese drivers are like "bots" – very predictable, safe, and law-abiding (sometimes frustratingly slow, e.g., 70 km/h on a highway). They drive on the **left**. It takes about 15-20 minutes to get used to, except for the indicator and wiper stalks being reversed (expect to turn on wipers when you mean to signal!).
        *   **Hitchhiking:** I didn't do it. I met a European traveler who did it successfully, even getting offers for places to stay. It's an option, but perhaps something to consider once you're more comfortable and confident.
    *   **Itinerary & Sights:**
        *   Plan for about **two weeks** in Hokkaido; there's plenty to do.
        *   There are roughly four main areas to explore.
        *   **Otaru:** A charming town, easy day trip from Sapporo.
        *   **Daisetsuzan National Park:** Great for treks. If weather is bad, there are flower farms and markets in the area (easy to access with a car).
        *   **Beer Factories:** Asahi beer has a factory in Asahikawa; Sapporo beer has one in Sapporo. (Kirin is the other major Japanese beer).
        *   **Shiretoko National Park:** The most beautiful place I visited in Hokkaido (considering the season I was there).
            *   **Bear Season:** You will likely need to hire a guide. They are experienced and provide "scare lectures" and explain what to do if you encounter a bear. There's debate about the effectiveness of bear bells (some say they attract, some say they deter).
    *   **Getting to Hokkaido:**
        *   **Do NOT take the bullet train (Shinkansen) to Sapporo from Honshu.**
        *   **Domestic flights** are dramatically cheaper and faster for this distance.
        *   The bullet train is generally overrated in my opinion – it's essentially a spacious, expensive plane. Especially for a 2-month trip, the cost difference for multiple long journeys will be huge. You might spend 4 hours instead of 2.5 on a journey, but it could cost a third of the price.

### **4. Tokyo (טוקיו)**
*Tags: #tokyo #tokyostay #shinjuku #shibuya #ginza #BeitHaSamuraiLocation #tokyoparks #climbingtokyo #transporttokyo #metropolis*

*   I spent two months in Tokyo and could talk about it for an hour. It’s an incredible place.
*   **Weather:** As mentioned, July/August is brutal: 30°C with 90% humidity. I experienced a full month of this (mid-July to mid-August), but by then I was "living" there, not frantically sightseeing.
*   **Tokyo is a Metropolis, Not a City:**
    *   Don't be fooled by accommodation listings saying "close to the city center." Ask, "Which city center?" Tokyo is vast.
    *   Travel times can be significant, but public transport is excellent. For perspective, Shinjuku to Akihabara can be 25-30 minutes by train, similar to distances across a large urban area like Gush Dan if it had efficient rail.
*   **Where to Stay:**
    *   **Good Central Areas:** Shinjuku, Shibuya (can be a bit expensive), Ginza (if you're less into nightlife and more into general exploring).
    *   **Beit HaSamurai (The Samurai House):**
        *   Run by a wonderful Japanese Messianic Jewish man (Isaac) and his Jewish wife (Melissa). They host Israelis in an upstairs section of their home for a very affordable price (around 50 NIS/night when I was there). They also host Friday night dinners. I adore them.
        *   **Contact them in advance via Instagram** if you're interested in staying or joining a Friday dinner.
        *   **Location:** It's **far from the central areas** mentioned above. Expect a 30 to 50-minute train journey to get to most popular spots. It's in a quiet, residential neighborhood.
        *   **Social Aspect:** As noted in "Solo Travel in Japan," you'll primarily be with other Israelis. If you don't make an effort or know the family beforehand, you might not interact much with the hosts themselves, as guests are mostly upstairs. The real "feature" for me was spending time with the family.
*   **What I Did in Tokyo (during the hot month):**
    *   Mostly **parks and cafes.**
    *   **Tokyo's Parks are Amazing:** Even though Tokyo is a "concrete jungle," its parks are incredible. You step inside and genuinely feel like you're in nature.
        *   They are designed to be orderly yet allow parts to be wild and untamed between the paths. It's not like the highly manicured style of Central Park in New York.
        *   You can be surrounded by trees and not even hear the city noise. I loved them.
*   **Climbing:**
    *   Tokyo (and Japan in general) has a **world-class climbing scene.** Japanese climbers are on another level.
    *   Even if you're not at their level, it's a great place to climb (e.g., if you do V6 elsewhere, you might do their V2-V4, but it's still fun and challenging).

*   **Driving Culture:** As mentioned, Japanese drivers are very orderly and law-abiding, like "bots."
*   **Attitudes Towards Israelis:** (Repeated from "Solo Travel in Japan" for emphasis) Based on my experience traveling after October 7th, locals genuinely don't seem to care or make an issue of you being Israeli.

### **6. Champion Tips (טיפים של אלופים)**
*Tags: #pro-tips #budget #transporttips #accommodationtips #drivingpermit #planning*

*   **International Driving Permit (IDP):** Get one. It's cheap, quick, and essential if you plan to rent a car (e.g., Optica Halperin in Israel).
*   **Transportation - Flights vs. Bullet Train:** For long distances (e.g., Tokyo to Sapporo), domestic flights are significantly cheaper than the Shinkansen (bullet train). The Shinkansen is often romanticized but is expensive for what it is.
*   **Beit HaSamurai (Tokyo):** A fantastic budget-friendly accommodation option with a warm, welcoming family and the bonus of Shabbat dinners if you're looking for that connection. Book well in advance via their Instagram. Be mindful of its location and the predominantly Israeli guest demographic if you're seeking other types of social interaction.
*   **Climbing:** If you're a climber, Japan is a top-tier destination. Research gyms and outdoor spots.
*   **Itinerary Flexibility (especially in summer):** Prioritize cooler regions like Hokkaido or the Japanese Alps during July/August. If you must be in cities like Tokyo, build in lots of indoor activities or park visits.
*   **Renting Cars:** Don't just check major rental websites; look for local Japanese car rental companies, as they can sometimes offer better deals, especially outside major tourist hubs.
